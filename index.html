Hybrid Retrieval & Classification for Climate Fact-Checking

Status: Complete

NLP · Evidence retrieval & claim verification

Context

Climate misinformation is hard to verify at scale. This project builds an automated fact-checking system that checks claims against a 1.2M-passage evidence corpus, built and benchmarked entirely on a single free Google Colab GPU, with no dedicated compute budget.

Goal

Given a climate-related claim, retrieve the supporting evidence and classify it as SUPPORTS, REFUTES, NOT_ENOUGH_INFO, or DISPUTED.

Approach
  • Built a 3-stage pipeline that finds likely evidence, double-checks the strongest matches, then classifies the claim (technically: BERT-based retrieval, Cross-Encoder re-ranking, and a BERT classifier)
  • Identified the retrieval stage as the main bottleneck: keyword search (BM25) found only 42–55% of the correct evidence, regardless of later steps. Switching to semantic search (matching meaning, not just keywords) raised that ceiling to 70.6%; testing 5 such models, a general-purpose one beat science-specific ones by 6–9 points, the opposite of what I expected
  • Cross-Encoder reranking: chose a smaller candidate pool (100 to 200) over a larger one (300 to 500) for two reasons. It's the most expensive step in the pipeline, so on a free, time-boxed Colab GPU session, a smaller pool keeps the whole thing runnable; and once the first-stage retriever is decent, most of the truly relevant evidence is already inside the top 100 to 200, so a larger pool mostly adds irrelevant candidates rather than useful signal
  • Fine-tuned and compared 4 different classifier setups, correcting for the fact that some claim categories had far fewer training examples than others (using techniques like weighting rare classes more heavily and oversampling them)
Result

Improved the system's ability to find the right evidence, raising its retrieval score from 0.139 to 0.196, and lifted the overall task score (which combines retrieval and classification accuracy) from 0.185 to 0.253.

  • Lesson: a class with near-zero recall is worth treating as a potential bug in the pipeline, not just an accepted weak point. It's worth tracing back through every stage before assuming the model has simply hit a real ceiling
  • Next step: I tuned hyperparameters across the existing methods, but suspect a real fix needs more than tuning. Structural changes I haven't tried yet but would like to explore: better data preprocessing, small LLMs, hierarchical classifiers, CNNs, or attention pooling

Zebrafish Tissue Segmentation: Deep Learning Capstone

Status: In Progress

Computer Vision · 2-semester capstone (2026) · Partners: Melbourne Data Analytics Platform (MDAP) & Peter MacCallum Cancer Centre, University of Melbourne

Context

Zebrafish are a model organism for studying liver cancer and an associated wasting syndrome, cancer-associated cachexia (CAC), which causes severe loss of muscle and fat in patients and currently has no therapies. This capstone is part of an official University of Melbourne research collaboration with the Peter MacCallum Cancer Centre, building an automated deep learning pipeline (object detection and semantic segmentation) to replace slow, manual screening of micro-CT scans for cachexia indicators in liver, adipose, and pore tissue.

Goal

In preparation for the implementation phase, my Semester 1 goal was to evaluate the existing pipeline against expert-annotated ground truth and identify exactly where it needed improvement before any changes were made.

Approach
  • Evaluated inference results against expert-annotated ground truth using Dice/IoU metrics computed per tissue class
  • Ran stratified evaluations across different tissue-density regions instead of treating every slice equally, since many contain only background with no tissue present, which would otherwise inflate accuracy scores
  • Built a mask-splitting workflow to decompose combined multi-class outputs into per-class binary masks, enabling downstream analysis such as tissue volume quantification
Result

Strong performance on larger, well-defined tissue structures like the liver. Accuracy drops noticeably on small, sparse structures like pores, primarily due to missed detections, which traces back to the detection stage (Stage 1) rather than the segmentation stage (Stage 2), an insight that reframed where Semester 2 effort goes.

Stack
YOLOv8 (Ultralytics) U-Net (PyTorch) Python (NumPy, pandas, PIL) SLURM-managed HPC GPU cluster Custom Dice/IoU tooling

Specific dataset, infrastructure, and result figures are kept confidential here, beyond what's published officially. Happy to discuss the technical approach in more depth in an interview.
